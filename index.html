The Internet
As A Puzzle

The internet contains a plethora of information, data and knowledge. It is home to a number of internet communities and niches each with their own specifc slang and jargon. Looking in from the outside, understanding this language might be confusing.

However, despite this, and despite any differences these communities might have, at the very heart of the internet, understood by and uniting all these different people, are memes.

Ideation: 6 October

When first approaching this project I was going to try and develop my work from project three, and meld it into something fitting for this project. For project 3 I created a random recipe generator, where users would be able to upload their own recipes and generate random recipes from other people. However I struggled to translate this to a physical form, and I felt like it wasn't particularly representative of the internet as a whole

Instead I decided to take this oppurtunity to bring to life something which truly represented the internet.

I came up with three initial ideas - a physical calendar or wall planner which had a different meme for every month of the year, a puzzle of memes, and a flip book which played out a popular vine as you flipped through it.

Since this project required we design for an audience, I decided not to do a calendar. A wall planner would have more interaction but I still felt like it wasn't really audience based.

A flipbook would be incredibly interactive, relying completely on an audience/user for it to function, except I didn't want to distill the essence of the internet down to just one, max two vines.

So I decided to develop a jigsaw puzzle with memes as the picture. Jigsaws are interactive and like flip books require an audience to function. With a jigsaw puzzle I would also be able to include lots of different memes rather than one or two.



Concept

Internet Yami-Ichi focuses around the idea of "browsing" face to face, and translating elements of the internet into physical forms. I decided to create something that would fit into this category. A jigsaw puzzle is interactive, and can be used by an individual or a group of people socially, just as the internet can be.


Memes are essentially a form of communication, and being largely picture based, can be easily understood by people no matter what language they speak. Jigsaw puzzles are a simple concept, and like memes, are a visual medium which do not require words to understand how they work and how to interact with them. Therefore I thought it was quite fitting to create a meme jigsaw puzzle.

My concept for this project is a Meme Jigsaw Puzzle, a jigsaw puzzle made up of memes. I wanted to select memes from the internet which were considered classic and would be recognised by nearly everyone . The memes I chose were ones that I have personally seen and interacted with on the internet with great frequency, so I do acknowledge they might differ to the memes someone else would consider popular. I essentially wanted to distill internet culture down into a puzzle.


Process - Initial Designs: 7 October

I looked online for popular memes, especially on knowyourmeme.com which was immensely helpful. I chose some which I felt were particularly iconic and popular, and which I had seen with great frequency all over the internet over the last several years.

I mocked up the puzzle designs on Adobe Photoshop, going for a layered and cluttered look for the front of the puzzle. On a different layer I added a puzzle template so I would know where to cut the pieces. I did the same for the back of the puzzle.

Printing & Paper: 7 October

At university I printed out several copies of my puzzle designs on A3 printing paper, and also went and bought some card from Gordon Harris for the puzzle backing. I considered making my puzzle bigger, however for ease of printing and presentation I decided to keep it at an A3 size.

I later realised I did not need the puzzle template on the back design and removed it in Photoshop, then went back and printed some copies without the template.

The first printed copies and the card.

Piecing it Together I: 8 October

With everything printed and all the supplies I needed, I started to make the puzzle. I glued down the two sides of the puzzle onto the card and made sure there were as few air bubbles as possible. Once this was dry, I began to cut out the puzzle pieces using a pair of scissors and nail scissors.

After cutting out a few pieces, I realised that the puzzle would look a lot better without the black divider lines. So I went back into Photoshop and lowered the opacity so they were barely visible, but just enough that I could figure out where to cut the pieces. At this point I also added in a couple more memes to the design, this time more recent ones to cover a wider range of internet culture.

During this initial process of cutting out puzzle pieces I also realised that using scissors to cut the puzzle pieces was very inefficient and quite messy, so I also went out and bought a scalpel to use instead.

Piecing it Together II: 8 October

After printing out the new copies and buying a scalpel, I got back to making the puzzle. The scalpel made it much easier to get the precise puzzle shapes, but it was still quite a tedious process.

The new design printed and glued onto card backing.

Tedious progress with the new puzzle

Using a gluestick to glue down the puzzle design to the card meant it took a lot of glue to stick the paper down properly. With many of the pieces the edges peeled up at the sides, so I had to tidy up many of the pieces at the end.

Some edge detail - Even using a scalpel it was difficult to get perfect edges. Cutting through the two pieces of paper and the card meant often one of them got a little damaged.
If I were to approach this project again I would try out cutting the card first and then the paper so less damage would be done to the actual puzzle designs.

Conclusion - Complete Project: 9 October

This is the finished puzzle. I am quite happy with how it turned out. It carried out the concept I wanted it to, being representative of universal internet culture and communication.

This project was quite challenging particularly because it was working with physical objects rather than digitally. A lot of things during the process took much longer than I expected. Cutting out the puzzle pieces was incredibly time consuming and fiddly. It was difficult to make the puzzle pieces perfectly clean cut. If I were to approach this differently I would set aside some time to learn how to use the laser cutter to get more precise pieces.

I also should have been more prepared with how many copies of each side I would need to print out, to make room for mistakes. Also related to printing, I initially printed from an iMac to one printer, which later ran out of A3 paper. When this happened I went downstairs to print from a Windows computer. The sizing and settings printed from here were slightly different and it was more difficult to get the settings I wanted. This is also something I would keep in mind in the future. If my time management had been better I would have liked to spend more time making the puzzle more polished and professional.

Despite all this, overall I am happy with the result of this project, there were no major roadblocks or changes and I was able to carry out my original concept.
